# The-Key-Shop-V2
V2 of The Key Shop with improved functionality and design

## Inspiration
The Key Shop is my little slice of the internet where I pretend I am living the dream as a seller of all things keyboard related. A couple years ago I realized V1 (which you can view [here](https://thekeyshop.onrender.com/) ). This was my first time working on an eCommerce application without prompting and also my first time putting into practice ideas related to design. In this initial implementation, I was focused on showing my ability to integrate frontend and backend using MERN. I think there were some aspects that went well, and others (such as not understanding fully how to deploy a server using render as the host, which still results in multiple error emails about the api being down) that did not. The resulting website served as a foundational stepping stone. A few years later and more lessons learned, I decided to revisit The Key Shop to give update the visuals and functionality, and also to remove the backend integration and narrow in on what makes a client delightful to use.

## Planning and Design 
There are a couple of goals intermingling in the conception and ideation of The Key Shop's second iteration. I want to use this as an opportunity to delve deeper into not only the functionality and code structure, but also the UX design, being more thoughtful about layout and accessibility. 

![PNG image displaying wireframe of Landing page. Various sized rectangles are used as placeholders for icons, images, and text. The initial wireframe outlines the Landing page containing 3 parts, a hero section with a call to action button, a categories section for users to navigate to a specific category of products, and an about us section at the bottom, with placeholders for newsletter signup and options to connect with the company.](./the-key-shop-v2/src/assets/The%20Key%20Shop%20V2.png)

The first version of The Key Shop was heavily influenced by websites hosted by CannonKeys, and Novelkeys, both sites I had noted leaned into heavily using blue, which from my knowledge well, I know can be good with symbolizing trust and safety. This time around I wanted to lean away froom blue and pure white. One company I've always enjoyed from their products to their website UX/UI experience is Osume. 

Osume does a great job on a couple different fronts. Primarily what stood out to me is their font types, muted color choice, and minified thumbnail design that carried over into product information.
